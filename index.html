01
AI Assurance, Risk & Governance
Regulation · Compliance · Audit Frameworks
In Short
Building next-generation assurance systems for regulated industries – moving from deterministic checklists to probabilistic, resilience-based frameworks. Developing the AI Assurance Framework for pharma and pioneering audit-as-set-cover methodologies with cascade-blocking models.
The Challenge
Traditional audit relies on static checklists and binary pass/fail judgments. But in pharma – where a single AI model might influence drug safety decisions for millions of patients – we need assurance that is continuous, probabilistic, and structurally aware. How do you audit something that learns?
From Checklists to Topology
My approach treats the audit universe as a set-cover problem: each control mitigates a subset of risks, and the optimal audit plan is the minimum collection of controls that covers the full risk space. I model control effectiveness as a decaying exponential – controls erode over time through organizational drift, software updates, and personnel changes. When a control drops below a threshold θ, its risk set becomes uncovered, potentially triggering cascading exposures in downstream controls. This transforms audit planning from a static exercise into a dynamic optimization problem with deep connections to anomaly detection in financial records, Bayesian operational risk modeling, and percolation theory from statistical physics. I'm developing this framework in a Substack series on AI governance and the open-source auditcover package.
Why This Matters to Me
I came to audit from fields where you can't afford to be wrong about your models. In pharma, the stakes are even higher: a flawed AI system in pharmacovigilance can miss safety signals that affect real patients. I find the intellectual challenge of designing assurance systems for adaptive AI deeply compelling. It's where the hardest problems in formal verification meet the messiest realities of enterprise software. The question isn't just "does this model work?" – it's "will this model keep working as the world shifts beneath it?"

02
Mathematical Foundations of AI/ML
Geometry · Topology · Information Theory
In Short
Applying deep mathematical structures – differential geometry, algebraic topology, persistent homology, and Ricci curvature – to real-world machine learning and data quality problems. Treating data integration as manifold gluing where curvature singularities encode risk.
The Challenge
Current AI runs on brute-force matrix multiplication: GPT-4's training consumed ~50 GWh – enough to power a small city for a year. Meanwhile, the human brain performs 10¹⁵ synaptic operations per second on 20 watts. This 10⁶× efficiency gap isn't just an engineering problem – it's a sign that our computational paradigm is fundamentally misaligned with the structure of intelligence. What would AI look like if we built it on geometry, sparsity, and biological principles instead of dense linear algebra?
Why Current Models Are Suboptimal
Transformers treat every token equally, attending to every other token in a quadratic explosion of computation. Biological neural networks do the opposite: they are massively sparse, event-driven, and compute only when needed. Spiking neural networks (SNNs) – the third generation of neural networks – encode information in the timing of discrete spikes rather than in continuous-valued activations. On neuromorphic hardware like Intel Loihi 2, SNNs achieve 50–300× energy reduction over GPU baselines for inference tasks. This isn't incremental improvement – it's a paradigm shift. The question is whether we can combine this biological efficiency with the representational power of geometric deep learning.
Brain-Inspired Architectures Meet Geometric Learning
The convergence I'm most excited about is between neuromorphic computing and geometric deep learning. SNNs naturally encode temporal structure through spike timing – but they lack the topological awareness that persistent homology provides. Conversely, TDA extracts shape from static point clouds but doesn't exploit the event-driven sparsity that makes biological computation efficient. Combining them – topologically-guided spiking architectures that attend only to geometrically significant features – could yield models that are both representationally powerful and energetically viable. Carlsson's foundational work on TDA provides the mathematical scaffolding; surrogate gradient methods for training SNNs provide the algorithmic bridge.
Geometric Data Science – Applied
Concretely, I apply persistent homology to FDA adverse event data for topological safety signal detection – persistent 1-cycles in the co-occurrence graph of adverse events identify drug-reaction clusters invisible to frequency-based methods. I use Ollivier-Ricci curvature on control networks to find structurally fragile audit pathways (negative curvature = bottleneck). And I frame data integration across clinical systems as a sheaf-theoretic consistency problem where local-to-global obstructions reveal systemic data quality failures that no row-level validation can catch. The broader vision: information geometry (Fisher-Rao manifolds of probability distributions) as a natural language for measuring control drift, model divergence, and data quality degradation over time.
Why This Matters to Me
I spent years studying the mathematical structures that describe how physical systems organize themselves – from neural networks in the brain to quantum field theories. The same tools that detect topological phase transitions in physics can detect structural anomalies in data pipelines. This isn't an analogy – it's the same mathematics. I'm drawn to this work because I believe the next leap in AI won't come from scaling compute alone, but from building models that understand the geometry of the problems they're solving. That's where my physics training becomes a genuine edge.

03
AI for Pharma & Diagnostics
Drug Discovery · Clinical Operations · Patient Outcomes
In Short
Applying machine learning and advanced analytics to accelerate drug discovery, improve clinical trial design, strengthen pharmacovigilance, and bring diagnostic precision closer to the patient. Moving pharma from retrospective reporting to predictive, real-time intelligence.
The Challenge
Bringing a new drug to market takes 10–15 years and costs over $2 billion on average. Clinical trials fail at a 90% rate, often due to poor patient stratification, late-detected safety signals, or flawed endpoint selection. Meanwhile, pharma sits on vast, underexploited datasets – electronic health records, adverse event reports, genomic profiles, imaging archives – that could transform every stage of the pipeline, from target identification to post-market surveillance. The bottleneck isn't data availability; it's the analytical capability to extract actionable insight at scale under strict regulatory constraints.
Where ML Is Changing the Game
The highest-impact applications of AI in pharma compound quietly across the entire value chain. In drug discovery, foundation models like Boltz-2 and AlphaFold 3 predict protein structure and binding affinity from sequence alone, enabling virtual screening of thousands of candidates before a single molecule is synthesized. In clinical operations, NLP models parse millions of adverse event narratives from the FDA FAERS database to detect safety signals months earlier than traditional disproportionality analysis. In diagnostics, Google's MedGemma achieves radiologist-level chest X-ray report generation, while pathology foundation models are transforming tissue analysis at scale. In federated learning, NVIDIA Clara enables multi-hospital model training without patient data ever leaving institutional boundaries – critical for privacy-preserving AI in regulated environments. In clinical trial design, agentic AI systems built on MCP + FHIR are beginning to automate care gap detection, documentation auditing, and real-time patient stratification on synthetic and real EHR data.
From Structure to Treatment: Drug Repurposing
The convergence of structural biology and pharmacogenomics opens a powerful axis: take a patient's genetic variant, predict the mutant protein structure, and screen existing FDA-approved drugs for repurposing opportunities. Boltz-2 predicts both structure and binding affinity (r ≈ 0.66 on the FEP+ benchmark – approaching physics-based simulation accuracy at 1,000× the speed), making virtual repurposing screens feasible at scale. Combined with topological data analysis to map drug-reaction clusters in adverse event data, this creates a feedback loop: population-level safety signals inform individual-level treatment hypotheses, and vice versa.

Bridging Healthcare & Technology
Switzerland · Europe · Innovation Ecosystem
The Thesis
The next wave of healthcare innovation won't come from pharma or tech alone – it will come from people and institutions that operate across both. Switzerland concentrates all the ingredients within 200 km: global pharma HQs, world-class AI labs, a progressive regulator, and deep-tech capital. I'm working to connect these worlds through cross-sector partnerships and a healthcare AI sandbox model, starting here and scaling across Europe.
The Challenge
Pharma and tech speak different languages. Pharma optimizes for safety and regulatory certainty over decades; tech optimizes for speed and iteration over weeks. Europe has world-class science and the strongest regulatory framework (EU AI Act, MDR, IVDR) – but risks becoming a rule-writer rather than an innovator. Who builds the bridge?
Switzerland: 200 km of Concentrated Innovation
Switzerland hosts a uniquely concentrated innovation corridor. The biotech sector generated CHF 7.2 billion in revenue in 2024 and raised a record CHF 2.5 billion in capital. Over 40% of current Swiss drug-discovery projects apply machine learning. What makes this ecosystem exceptional isn't just scale – it's proximity. Basel pharma headquarters, Zürich AI labs, Bern computational neuroscience, Lausanne deep-tech research, and Geneva digital health are all within three hours of each other. No other region packs this density of complementary expertise into such a small geographic footprint.

The Sandbox Vision
Switzerland's unique position makes it the natural place to prototype a healthcare AI sandbox: a pre-competitive space where pharma, tech companies, regulators, and academics can test AI systems on real clinical data under controlled governance, before deployment. The ingredients already exist – Basel's DayOne Accelerator connects startups with pharma, the SDSC (ETH/EPFL joint initiative) provides data infrastructure, Swissmedic in Bern offers regulatory proximity, and the Wyss Centers in Zürich and Geneva bridge translational gaps. The fintech sandbox model (Singapore, UK) has already proven that regulated innovation spaces accelerate adoption without compromising safety. Healthcare is next.
